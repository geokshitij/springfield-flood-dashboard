Flood Resilience in Springfield Township

Springfield Township's 2024 Comprehensive Plan recommended the Township "assess stormwater infrastructure and capacity to address community needs," in a climate where the rainfall numbers the current ordinances were built on no longer match what the township actually receives. This page summarizes what we (the AGU Thriving Earth Exchange science team paired with the Township) found, and the top-priority stormwater management issues the findings point to.

Bottom line for the Supervisors. Today's largest one-day storms are already running about a quarter above the federal regulatory rainfall the ordinances reference, and the climate-model ensemble points to roughly another tenth on top by late century. We recommend the Township consider two changes: design new stormwater work for an 11-inch one-day storm, and prioritize resources on these three Cooks Creek road crossings.

Look at the map first

Warmer colors mark places more likely to flood than the township average. Open circles are the six road sites the Township already flagged in its Act 167 list; the yellow star is the Springtown drinking-water well. Toggle layers and click a marker for its underlying numbers.

The map's underlying method, layers, and weights are explained in Where road flooding risk concentrates and Inputs and weights.

Why this matters now

Springfield Township covers about 24 of the 30 square miles of the Cooks Creek Watershed. The current floodplain maps and stormwater ordinances were built on rainfall statistics from before the recent clustering of extreme storms. Hurricane Irene (August 2011) and Hurricane Ida (September 2021) each dropped roughly seven inches of rain on the township in a day, and Ida alone caused about $90,000 in township-wide damage. Both storms would have been smaller events in the historical rainfall record the current rulebook assumes.

The upcoming Act 167 stormwater ordinance revisions are a natural moment to bring those numbers up to date and to put the next round of stormwater capital spending where the data say the risk concentrates. We prepared this page using only public data and code that anyone can rerun, so a follow-on engineering study or grant application can build on every number here without redoing the analysis from scratch.

What we found, at a glance

Today's heaviest storms vs. the rulebook +25%
The biggest one-day rainstorm the township should plan for has grown to about 10.3 inches, against the 8.2 inches in the federal regulatory reference (NOAA Atlas-14) the ordinances currently cite.
Add late-century climate change +10%
A 7-model climate ensemble (CMIP6) points to roughly another +10% in extreme daily rainfall by 2055-2084. Combine that with today's number and a forward-looking design target is around 11.3 inches in a day.
Three of six known road flood sites stand out 3 of 6
Bridge St & 212, North Mine Rd, and Hickon Rd come out on top of the susceptibility ranking. All three are road crossings of Cooks Creek tributaries with substantial upstream drainage.

Top priority stormwater management issues

Each issue below links to the section of this page that carries its supporting evidence; each is sized to be folded into ordinance language or the next capital cycle without further study.

  • Update the design-storm value used in the stormwater and floodplain ordinances. The current 8.2 inch (Atlas-14) reference is below what the station record already shows; once climate uplift is included, a roughly 11 inch one-day rainfall is what the data support for forward-looking infrastructure. Evidence: Design rainfall + Climate uplift.
  • Prioritize resources on these three Cooks Creek road crossings. Bridge St & 212, North Mine Rd, and Hickon Rd are the three sites where the susceptibility analysis most strongly agrees with the Township's own flood-concern list. The other three sites flag a different failure mode (local stormwater on small drainages) that needs its own workstream. Evidence: Where road flooding risk concentrates + Priority sites.

The 34-year record at this one station is too short to detect a trend, but that is not "no change"

Statistical tests on the 32 complete years of Springtown rainfall show no significant trend in annual totals, annual maxima, or counts of heavy-rain days at the usual significance level. The record is simply too short to detect the regional climate signal at one gauge. Climate models, looked at separately in Climate uplift, do show that signal clearly. The right reading of the table below is "we have not proven a trend at this gauge yet," not "the storms are not getting bigger."

Climate uplift

On top of the already-larger storms in today's record, a seven-model climate ensemble suggests the typical annual extreme rainfall at the township grows by roughly another 5 to 10 percent by the second half of this century. We recommend folding that uplift into any new stormwater infrastructure design, so it does not need to be rebuilt before the end of its design life.

Why we use the typical-extreme number rather than the rarest-of-the-rare. Climate models can also be asked directly about the 100-year storm itself, but with only 30 years per model in each future window the answer wobbles widely across models (anywhere from a -22% drop to a +28% rise depending on the model). The typical annual extreme is a much steadier signal across models and is what we recommend the Township use as a planning uplift.

Where road flooding risk concentrates

The map below points to the same flood-prone places the Township already knows from experience, which is the validation test that matters most. It is a relative ranking of vulnerability built from elevation, distance to streams, soils, and land cover; it does not predict how deep the water would get in any particular storm.

How to read the susceptibility score. The score is relative, and ranked only within Springfield Township (plus a small surrounding buffer). It is not a probability and not a water depth. The five color bands are defined as fifths of the township, so a place in the Very High band is among the most susceptible 20% of Springfield, not a place certain to flood, and a score near 1 means "about as susceptible as it gets in Springfield," not "guaranteed to flood." Because every input layer is scaled within Springfield, the score cannot be compared across towns: our 1 and a neighboring township's 1 do not mean the same thing.

Where the single highest-scoring point in the township sits

Not one of the pre-flagged sites: searching the full raster for its true maximum finds it in the township's southwestern corner, inside the Highway Commercial zoning district next to the Development District, the township's commercial/development corridor. Susceptibility there peaks at 0.88, and the roughly 24-acre Very-High cluster around it sits outside the FEMA-mapped floodplain. Zoning and susceptibility agree on this corner; FEMA does not, worth weighing in any future review of development there. Details in Sensitive receptors.

Suggested pad height. Mount the emergency generator at least 8 to 10 feet above the normal Cooks Creek water surface at the well-yard. That figure stacks 4 to 7 feet for the expected creek rise in a 100-year storm on a ~4-square-mile drainage, plus 2 feet of conventional freeboard, plus another half-foot to foot-and-a-half for climate uplift.
One field measurement would sharpen this from a rule of thumb to an engineering number. A Township staff member or the consulting engineer measuring from the existing generator pad up to (a) the normal creek surface, (b) the Ida 2021 high-water mark, and (c) the Irene 2011 high-water mark would let us turn the 8-to-10-foot range into a single defensible value.

Buildings in the high-risk zones

Springfield has kept almost everything out of the mapped FEMA floodplain, yet about 1,470 buildings, most of them homes, fall in the High or Very-High susceptibility bands just outside it. This answers a question the Township raised: how many, and what kind of, buildings sit in the high-risk areas. It is a screening count from building footprints, not a record of buildings that have actually flooded.

Most flagged buildings sit just outside the mapped FEMA floodplain

Only about two dozen buildings in the whole township fall inside the FEMA 1%-annual-chance floodplain (15 of them in the High/Very-High bands). The other 1,457 high-risk buildings are outside the mapped floodplain, and 257 of those sit within 300 feet of its edge (71 within 100 feet). The current FEMA panels here reflect historic conditions, so this screen is flagging terrain-and-drainage exposure the regulatory map may not capture, the kind of place worth a closer look before it is treated as low risk.
What this count is, and is not. It is a relative screen, not a list of buildings that have flooded. We have no Township record of which buildings flood, which is why this is a follow-on worth doing properly (for example, joining confirmed flooded-property addresses, if the Township can assemble any). A building in the Very-High band is in the most susceptible fifth of Springfield, not certain to flood.

Village zoning (VR/VC): the population-center proxy

Springfield's 4 named villages (VR and VC zoning combined per village) score Moderate to Very High on average. Pleasant Valley and Zion Hill carry the most risk (100% and 86% combined High or Very High); Springtown and Passer are comparatively lower (about 20% and 18%). These receptors are point samples or zone averages of the same relative susceptibility score used everywhere else on this page, not a hydraulic study of any specific structure. They do not change the priority issues at the top of this page; they extend the same evidence to more of the places the Township asked about.

Inputs and weights

The susceptibility score combines six public spatial layers; each layer's weight comes from a published study in a similar US or European watershed, not from our judgment. A consultant or peer reviewer can swap any weight and rerun the analysis from the project scripts.

Does the ranking depend on the weights we chose?

The three top sites stay the three top sites under every published weighting scheme we tested. Only the ordering of the lower three sites shifts with the choice of weights, and the direction of that shift is explainable from how each reference study weighted elevation.

What this dashboard cannot do for you

Two honest limits the Supervisors should know about before the recommendations on this page move into ordinance language or a capital plan. Each item below is framed in terms of what it means for the decision, with the technical reason underneath.

This map ranks where flooding is more likely, but does not tell you how deep the water will get

  • The susceptibility score sorts places relative to each other; it does not produce a water-surface elevation for any storm. For a specific elevation at a specific site, the consulting engineer will still need a site-specific hydraulic analysis. Read the Township's existing FEMA flood maps alongside this dashboard, not in place of them.
  • The underlying stream network resolves Cooks Creek and its bigger tributaries but is too coarse (about 90 metres per pixel) to capture the smallest local drainages. That is why some of the Township's flood-prone sites score Moderate rather than Very High; those sites likely flood from local stormwater rather than Cooks Creek itself.
  • Springfield's limestone karst drives sinkholes and rapid subsurface flow that no surface-water index captures. If a specific parcel under consideration is known to be karst-prone, the dashboard's score is incomplete for that parcel.
  • The climate uplift is applied only to the rainfall numbers, not to the spatial map. A future-flood map (today's map redrawn under tomorrow's rainfall) is a reasonable next deliverable but is not what is shown here.

The records we used are real but short

  • The Springtown rain-gauge record runs only 34 years, which is enough to estimate the size of today's design storms but not long enough to prove a statistically significant upward trend at that one gauge. The right reading of the trend table is "we have not proven a trend at this gauge yet," not "the storms are not getting bigger."
  • The 10% climate uplift is the most stable signal across seven CMIP6 models. If someone asks instead about the change in the 100-year storm itself, the same models disagree widely (the middle half of models lands anywhere from a 22% drop to a 28% rise). We rely on the more stable typical-extreme signal as the planning multiplier.
  • Seven climate models is a modest ensemble. Adding more would tighten the median estimate but is unlikely to change the qualitative finding of roughly 5 to 10% growth in extreme rainfall.

Suggested next steps

Two short actions, each sized to a single Township staff visit or a paragraph in ordinance language. Each item names who could lead it, what specifically we need from them, and how the team would know it landed.

  1. Tape measurement at the Springtown well-yard. Owner: a Township staff member or the consulting engineer. Ask: three measurements from the existing generator pad up to (a) the normal Cooks Creek water surface today, (b) the Hurricane Ida (Sept 2021) high-water mark if anyone can still identify it, and (c) the Hurricane Irene (Aug 2011) high-water mark if anyone has a reference. By when: before the well's generator specification is finalized. What we do with it: turn the 8 to 10 foot range in this memo into a single defensible pad-height number.
  2. Use roughly 11 inches as the planning one-day rainfall in new ordinance language. Owners: the Township engineer and the Bucks County Planning Commission. Ask: when the Act 167 Update is completed and ordinance revisions are requested by BCPC, reference the gauge-fit plus 10% climate uplift (roughly 11 inches per day) rather than the 8.2 inch federal default, and cite this dashboard as the basis. By when: during the ordinance drafting cycle. What we do with it: the design rainfall used in any new Township stormwater work is now in line with what the rain gauge and climate models both indicate.

What this dashboard is, and is not

This is a research-grade screening tool built by university scientists with the Springfield Township government and the Cooks Creek Watershed Association under the AGU Thriving Earth Exchange. It is not an engineering certification, a regulatory floodplain map, or a substitute for FEMA NFHL or a detailed hydraulic model. It is meant to inform priority-setting and to be used in conversation with the project team.
